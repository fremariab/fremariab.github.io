The Importance of the Systems Development Life Cycle


Sir Arthur Conan Doyle once said, “It has long been an axiom of mine that the little things are infinitely the most important”. Indeed the little things in life have the most significance (like my 5’5″ self!). This highlights how people focus on the product but not the process. For example, if a new exeat system would’ve been developed in SOS-HGIC, most people would pay particular attention to how the system has improved, rather than how the system came to be. Relating this example to the quote, it is evident that in developing a new system, the most important characteristic of the system, unknown to many, is how it came to be, specifically through the Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC). The importance of the Systems Development Life Cycle can be evaluated in many areas. In this blog post, I am going to elaborate on some of these areas and relate it to the Systems Development Life Cycle.


End-users, individuals who the system was purposefully designed for, play key roles in the planning of new systems. When planning a new system, the project managers must think of the end-users in all the stages of the SDLC. It is essential as the system must meet all the requirements of the end-users and must work to the standards of the end-users. If this does not happen, all the money and resources spent on the new system will be futile and the project would be deemed as a project failure. This then connects to the SDLC, which makes it necessary for those developing the system to always have the end-user in mind. For example, in our proposed solution for the SOS-HGIC Exeat System, the usability and functionality as well as the speed of the approval process, were all taken into consideration with respect to the end-users. Initially, end-users had problems with the system about delays and losses. For instance, user documentation makes sure that the end-users have information on how the system works as well as some other background knowledge. End-users are thought of here since the system may be difficult to use. Another instance is user acceptance testing also known as beta testing. This is done to make sure that there is an ease of use when using the system in real conditions. The importance of the Systems Development Life Cycle is then prominent when evaluating the role of end-users when planning a new system.


In addition, the relevant stakeholders play a part in the planning process. A stakeholder is an individual interested in and is affected by the activities of an organisation. The SDLC ensures that stakeholders are informed about the stage the project is in as they invest their funds into the project and are affected by changes. For example if the system doesn’t work in the long run they will be affected. With the use of the SDLC, this can be avoided where the justification of possible solutions takes place. A SWOT analysis is carried out to see if the new system is profitable by making sure that the benefits outweigh the costs.


Utilitarianism, doing the greatest good for the greatest number brings out the importance of the Systems Development Life Cycle . When developing a new system, there are many processes involved like getting software for the system and following standardised procedures. For example in the SDLC, quality assurance methods like the Capability Maturity Model Integration ensures that the development team are following standardised practices at all times (Stuart Gray 320).It is essential that teams must follow copyright laws which will in turn benefit the end-users.


Nevertheless, as beneficial as the Systems Development Life Cycle is, it has its flaws as it has social and ethical issues connected to it. One issue that arises is the equality of access. The Systems Development Life Cycle suggests that users must employ some methods and use systems, like bug tracking systems and have different types of maintenance but the reality of the situation is that not all people or organizations have access to this.


All things considered, the Systems Development Life Cycle is important to a large extent as it avoids project failure and benefits the organisation in the long run.

Multimedia Manipulation

Picture this. You’re casually scrolling through Instagram, liking some posts that you come across but you get a bit bored so you start going through people’s stories. You keep tapping through stories and it seems everyone’s story is similar – they all have that one post. You’re immediately interested and want to know more about the post and its significance. I’m positive that this has happened to you quite a number of times. As you can see from the pictured situation, undeniably, IT influences the way we think and determines what we regard as valuable or important to a large extent. In this blog post, I will be justifying this assertion in the context of multimedia/digital media.


In order to reveal the influence of IT on the way we think and as well as what we consider to be valuable or important, we must first have an idea of the IT system in question which is multimedia. Multimedia refers to the “the use and integration of media such as text, images and graphic elements, to create digital products that are available online or offline” (International Baccalaureate 2010). Multimedia usually involves audio editing, animations, video editing and photo editing. These branches of multimedia all require the manipulation of alignment, colour management and resolution, and involve cropping, layering, and filtering (Stuart 2011). Multimedia has many areas of impact such as education and home and leisure, in which the primary stakeholders differ respectively.


In education, multimedia has to a large extent influenced the way we think as well as determine what we regard as valuable or important. In education multimedia is used to help simplify concepts and topics for particular subjects. For instance, in biology, multimedia is used to explain the process of photosynthesis in a much shorter time as compared to reading. In such a case, it is possible that in the video or picture perhaps, some particular text has been animated to have more emphasis which would be difficult to do in a book. The use of the animation has been used to place emphasis on a particular word indicating that that particular part of the process is important. This clearly shows how IT has influenced what we consider to be important. One social and ethical issue that is raised in this area of impact is people and machines and policies. Students, the primary stakeholders in this case, are now using multimedia to learn as it helps in explanations and in revision. Students now prefer to learn using multimedia as compared to physical textbooks. This suggests that they are now spending more time online which may turn out to have negative effects, as their interaction with IT can result in eyestrain and headaches. As a result, policies must come in to limit the screen time for students when using multimedia in such a light.


Moreover, looking at home and leisure, multimedia has to a large extent influenced the way we think as well as determine what we regard as valuable or important. In home and leisure, social media is a great example. Social media is one of the ways in which multimedia is shared frequently. For instance on twitter, many posts are used to share information. Recently, after the Special Anti-Robbery Squad in Nigeria started mistreating civilians, a number of Nigerians used multimedia to share information regarding the attacks. Thousands of twitter users shared the posts to raise awareness and get international attention which proved to be fruitful. The use of social media in such an instance, shows that indeed there is strength in numbers. With thousands of people retweeting “#ENDSARS” posts, many news agencies and individuals, would have been influenced by the opposition to the SARS unit, and would have started thinking in the same way. Not only that but also would have started to see the #ENDSARS protest as something of value and something significant.


However, this brings into question how the increasing global dominance of multimedia gives rise to an increasing uniformity of thinking. Using the example stated earlier, the #ENDSARS protests were used to raise awareness. More and more people, through the use of such multimedia, not only got to know about the situation in Nigeria, but also formed an opinion on the whole issue. This likened them to the many Nigerians who used social media to amplify their cry for help. In doing so, they gained more support from people who thought the same way and who had the same beliefs – that the SARS unit should be dissolved. With more people thinking alike and seeing the benefits of the use of multimedia, many people will start to repost such posts and not credit the original creator. This sheds light on one of the most important social and ethical issues in multimedia, namely, intellectual property. This refers to “ideas, discoveries, writings, works of art, software, collections and presentations of data” (International Baccalaureate 2010). In order to ensure that the reposter does not take credit for the original work of the creator, the post will be copyrighted or watermarked to ensure that all credit goes where it is due.


Taking all things into consideration, it is evident that IT influences the way we think and determines what we regard as valuable or important to a large extent. By exploring a number of areas of impact, it is clear that multimedia in this case, can influence the way people think as well as show what is deemed as important. This shows how important technological developments in such a field are. One who understands such a statement, can use it to their benefit. For instance, governments use multimedia to campaign. One example is John Mahama using a generic design, a stock photo, for his presidential campaign logo. The multimedia was used to campaign but the editing was so generic it caused an uproar amongst some. This resulted in a number of people sharing the post to many and ultimately, sharing John Mahama’s campaign poster. This all displays our dependence on IT to share ideas and information but to what extent can we keep our independence as we have now seen that IT can influence our way of thinking.

Finding A Connection

How often do you find yourself Googling “synonym for ….”? Frequently? Well for me, I do that every single day when I do my homework. I’m sure we can all testify to how IT systems such as the internet have simplified many things in our everyday lives. From using the internet to write essays, to effectively communicating with others as well as selling products. With a wide range of uses, the issue of humans’ over reliance on the largest network in the world is brought to light.


The internet can be described as a “global wide area network that connects computer systems across the world.” This collection of networks allows other users to connect with people globally through the sharing of a variety of information. However, the use of this IT system influences the accessibility of information and the validity of such information.


For instance, the internet is used in a number of areas of impact such as education, home and leisure and health. In education, information from Mass Open Online Courses (MOOCs) can be accessed by students globally, provided that they have a device with stable internet connection. For instance in 2019, with 13,500 courses, the modern MOOC movement reached 120 million learners (Shah). In home and leisure, the internet is most commonly used in social networking software applications that allow users to create profiles and share content with other users on the platform. One famous example, Tiktok, has garnered over 5 billion downloads as of December 2020 (Iqbal). For health, information about the symptoms of different illnesses and diseases can be accessed on the internet. For example, according to Google Trends, search interest in the COVID-19 vaccine has reached an all-time high globally as of 11th March 2021 (Google Trends).


For these reasons, many internet users strive to have the best devices that allow them to access information more efficiently and rapidly. The movement of 3G users to LTE users is a prime display of humans’ over reliance on the internet. The number of LTE users increased from 3.9 billion in 2018 to 5.8 billion in 2021 (Statista). This need for faster bandwidth reveals the extent to which users depend on the internet.


With an understanding of the uses of the internet and the growing need for faster and reliable access to information on the internet, it is worthy to note that the quality of information found on the internet plays a large role. Undoubtedly, there is some information on the internet that is guaranteed to be reliable. Most internet users are under the impression that information on the internet is more likely to be up-to-date as web pages can be easily edited when new information comes about. This up-to-date information is easily accessible especially with the use of search engines. The introduction of web 2.0 technologies has also influenced the quality of information on the internet as they allow users to share content on the internet. If any ordinary internet user visits web 2.0 technologies such as blogs or wikis like Wikipedia, they are most likely to consider it as reliable due to it being “peer reviewed” in a sense. These factors have made the information available on the internet to be valid and reliable.


However, it must be highlighted that the information on the internet is far from perfect. The information on the internet does not go through any checking process and hence there are vast amounts of information that are biased, incorrect and inaccurate. Moreover, if search engines are not used properly, there is the possibility that users may be overloaded with irrelevant information. There is also the issue of accessing information on undesirable websites. For instance, users may connect to websites that are not genuine which could pose a number of security risks. These unresolved issues of credibility and undesirability are often overlooked by internet users.


Thus, when looking at the effects of such control, it can be said that humans have over time developed an over reliance on the internet. With easy access to a variety of information from anywhere in the world, internet users will often overlook the negative aspects of information accessed on the internet in an attempt to quickly get the information we need. It is for these reasons that human beings do not necessarily “believe these things to be true” but rather accept this information without questioning the validity and reliability of the information.


Taking all things into consideration, IT such as the internet, to some extent influences the accessibility of information and the reasons for blindly accepting this information, not thinking of its reliability or validity. This results in an over reliance on technology as humans are not used to not putting in effort to carry out tasks. This loosely relates to the concept of nomophobia, where users can not live without technology. In that regard, all internet users must start to monitor the degree to which they depend on technology in order to avoid such effects.


Still can’t see the impact of IT systems on the accessibility of information in our daily lives? My challenge to you today is to spend a whole day without using a search engine of any kind – it will reveal to you the intensity of our dependence on the internet. Maybe we should our quests for finding connections and look up from our devices make those connections ourselves.

The Usefulness of Facial Recognition

We live in a world where everything is developing a technological alternative. I recently chanced upon some articles that commented on the use of Artificial Intelligence in different areas of impact. They commented on the Chinese response to the application of facial recognition in helping make governmental decisions and the other made mention of the development of Artificial Intelligence to help African institutions in their provision of services. I am mainly interested in the role facial recognition software plays in different areas of impact. As a result, in this blog post, I will be evaluating the extent to which the usefulness of facial recognition software is dependent on the area of impact being considered.


To understand its usefulness we must first get an understanding of what facial recognition software is. This IT system seeks to “use biometrics to map facial features from a photograph or video and it compares the information with a database of known faces to find a match.” This branch of artificial intelligence has the appearance of human-like intelligence and it’s success in identity verification has allowed it to be used in a number of scenarios.


For instance, facial recognition software has been developed by a young girl in Ghana, namely Charlette N’Guessan, to help African institutions and industries that rely on the software in the provision of services. Her development of the software named BACE API, did not only make her win the 2020 Africa Prize for Engineering Innovation, but also helped these institutions, like Ghanaian banks, confront the pressing issue of cybercrime and identity theft. BACE API’s area of impact is business and employment and I believe it will have a positive and beneficial impact on the institutions that use it. However, it is crucial to note that all that glitters is not gold. Although in this situation, the use of facial recognition software appears to be fruitful, in other situations facial recognition software is being used “to track dangerous criminals and legitimate political protesters.” In this situation after a series of protests against the expedition of criminals to mainland China, the Chinese government has leveraged the surveillance systems they had in place and coupled with the facial recognition software to arrest protesters.


Evidently, the usefulness of facial recognition software varies depending on the area of impact being considered. Nonetheless, to make the argument more justifiable, we must consider the social and ethical concerns that come along with the use of the software. I perceive the development of the software, BACE API to aid in digital citizenship as with such a system in place, more people would have a more ethical and legal approach concerning the use of IT. On the contrary, the use of facial recognition software in Hong Kong, brings forth a number of social and ethical concerns such as privacy, surveillance, policies and anonymity. Protesters are upset that their privacy is not being respected. They believe that they have the right to determine for themselves when, how and to what extent information about themselves is being shared with others. Be that as it may, I find it very surprising that they expect their voices to be heard when they have turned around to do the exact same thing by leaking private information to government authorities.


This further encourages the act of surveillance as the government will want to not only monitor their actions again but use facial recognition to identify them and arrest them. The protesters believe that if the Hong Kong government says that there are privacy controls in place to govern the use of the software, then the government should ensure that the said controls are being implemented. The issue of policies arises because if there were enforceable measures created to monitor the inappropriate use relating to information technologies such as surveillance cameras, the use of facial recognition software and even using social media platforms, the situation would have been less catastrophic. It all boils down to authenticity; facial recognition software helps to establish a user’s identity beyond reasonable doubt. The only way this would be a concern is if individuals have done something bad and do not want to be held accountable.


All things considered, I believe that to a large extent, the usefulness of facial recognition software is dependent on the area of impact being considered. It is also worthy to note that the stakeholders involved also play a significant role in determining the usefulness of such an IT system. It is clear that policies are crucial in the use of facial recognition software because the stakeholders can both be held accountable if anything is to occur. In spite of that, as aforementioned, we live in a world where everything is developing a technological alternative and some technologies might oppose one another. What then would we do when making policies for those?

A Kind-of Expert Analysis on Expert Systems

Expert systems are those type of systems; the ones that think they know everything. Except they do…well to some extent. You see expert systems are programs or software that use human knowledge to mimic the expertise of an expert and solve a problem in a specific knowledge domain using specific rules.


They have over the years proven to be useful in many areas, such as education and training, medical diagnosis, financial decision-making and estate and insurance planning. One field that I find the use of expert systems particularly striking is in the financial field. In this blog post, I am going to evaluate the use of expert systems using the INVEX(Investment expert system) as a case study.


INVEX is a program that helps project analysts decide what projects to invest in. It works like any other expert system with a set of inference rules, a knowledge base and a user interface. It has an interactive user screen that is used to communicate with the user and the software asks the user or customer questions about their intentions and preferences. This information is used to build up a customer profile which would be used in the decision making process. The software uses Microsoft Excel for data entry, financial works as well as translations. Based on the information in the customer profile, the software will divide the investments into five groups namely: very bad, bad, medium, good and very good. It does this using values of dynamic parameters as seen below:


Investments from the group very good are accepted, while investments from the group very bad are rejected, before it takes part in multi-criteria decision-making. For the investments from the groups good, medium and bad, a group-specific sensitivity analysis is performed and then a user is asked whether to accept or reject each of these investments. The first step decreases the number of investments that will take part in multi-criteria decision-making, by rejecting the bad choices. Total pre-order used in multi-criteria decision-making gives the best investments from the set of accepted investments.


This financial expert system is useful to both project analysts and investors. Project analysts use INVEX to provide guidance to businesses on making investment decisions as it is less error-prone and time consuming. Investors also use INVEX to help them in deciding what investments will give them a greater return.


As much as the INVEX expert system is useful and helps in investment management, the use of this software brings about some social or ethical concerns. A few of these social or ethical concerns that emerge from the use of INVEX are reliability and security.


In some instances, the software is not protected from unauthorized access, paving a way for hackers to get access to the information. This is risky as when hackers get access to customer profiles they can use the information for other purposes which impacts the confidence in the system.


Moreover, at times the data does not completely correspond with the real world. In the financial field, there are often a lot of uncertainties. For example, using INVEX helps the managers assess future alternatives and the risk levels and return association between them. At times uncertain future investments are presented in terms of present consumption and resources and some uncertain factors include price and demand projections. As a result, reliability issues arise as prices may change and this may make the data outdated. It may even make the data less accurate since the parameters used to make decisions may be outdated.


These issues will impact the aforementioned stakeholders to a large extent. For project analysts, if the data is unreliable, the decisions that will be made to determine the viability of a project will not be good and will affect the business as they will make bad investments. This will cause the business to lose a lot of money and make even cost the project analyst his job. For the investors, if they invest in the investments that the software deems as good but are very bad in reality, they will make huge losses. Unreliability will impact the investor as well as decrease the value of the investment.


To combat these social or ethical concerns, a few solutions can be put forward. The knowledge engineer of the expert system can regularly review the data or parameters to update them. When they do this the data is kept up to date and is more reliable since it has a higher correspondence to the real world. Additionally, another way to combat the problem, is to introduce ranges for the parameters, prices and demand projections. Ranges that include future projections in uncertain factors such as price, can make it more reliable. This would reduce the likelihood of bad investments being deemed as acceptable.


In conclusion, by looking at INVEX, we can see that expert system are very useful but only to an extent due to the social and ethical concerns associated with it.

The Big Fat ‘F’ in Change Management

How would you feel if you invested millions of dollars into a new system only for it to end up faulty? Personally, I wouldn’t be moved because, really, what is a million dollars? That’s pocket change…which must appear in my pocket right now! Well, more often than not, many institutions that undergo change management of IT systems end up in project failure, losing millions of dollars in the process. Let me explain better.


In 2013, the Canadian Ministry of Children and Family Development spent $182 million (WHEW!) on a new and improved Integrated Case Management System to help protect sensitive data, improve service delivery and replace 64 different databases. This new system was meant to ensure that no vulnerable children were forgotten. Unfortunately, after spending all this money, the new system encountered many problems like constant breakdowns and some experienced difficulty using the system. In this blog post, I am going to evaluate the effects of change management of IT systems in an organisation, using the aforementioned situation, as a case study.


With such a task of handling thousands (probably millions) of files, the new system was supposed to work to the best standard and protect all the information that it stored. However it didn’t even launch for anyone to make comparisons of standard. It is noticeable that they lacked time management. Out of the eight recommendations to be evaluated, the group only evaluated six, leaving the other two and dubbing the system as incomplete. They did not utilize the time given to them from November 29th, 2010 till late 2014. They had 4 good years to implement the system and ensure that it would be successful yet it failed.


Moreover, project failure can be attributed to insufficient resources for training and the ignorance of the goals for the system. The Canadian Ministry of Children and Family Development had $182 million for the whole project yet could not attribute a small portion of the money to help train workers and get equipment for training. This brings to light another underlying problem which is money management. Allocation of money to the different aspects of the project, especially training, would have helped the project to be more successful as the workers would use it appropriately, reducing the time spent and making them more efficient in their work.


The Canadian Ministry of Children and Family Development started by purchasing modern software and partnering with Deloitte Incorporated to help them develop and implement the system. Artificial Intelligence : The Truth

As a child, I encountered many futuristic cartoons such as “My Life as A Teenage Robot” and “Robotboy”, which you might be familiar with. These shows presented technology as something visionary – something the world needed. I grew up to see indeed that in most aspects of the world, technology was being introduced. For example, about 4 years ago, my dad brought a “Roomba”, an automatic vacuum robot cleaner, into the house. Initially, I was excited that I would no longer have to spend time sweeping around the house as the Roomba was designed to do that, using machine learning. However, at a point in time, I found myself asking if I still know how to sweep, if I’ll ever sweep again and if Artificial Intelligence will replace humans. This incident inspired me to do some research about Artificial Intelligence and the myths that surround it. In this blog post, I will not only explore some of the myths that are often associated with Artificial Intelligence but also present the facts that address these myths.


Essentially, Artificial Intelligence involves a great deal of machine learning, a technique that makes computers produce better results by learning from past experiences (Stuart 330). This technique is related to the concept of computational intelligence, a concept which Artificial Intelligence has its basis. Most people have the preconceived notion that technology or Artificial Intelligence especially, is going to completely replace humans because it is ever evolving and as such will outrule the need for humans in specific fields. A great example is in manufacturing where Artificial Intelligence is used to accurately predict when a machine needs maintenance, also known as predictive maintenance (Kaja Polachowska). One may see this to be a great use of technology as it reduces machine downtime however others may see it as technology replacing control engineers. This myth needs to be flatlined. Artificial Intelligence can not replace humans and this is largely based on the fact that humans and computers are not the same. You might be thinking “well clearly they are different” but let’s look at this on a deeper level. Computers have good memory and better mathematical abilities than humans which explains why they are used in a number of fields but not all fields. This is because in some fields, there is not enough data for Artificial Intelligence to work in. Machine learning requires huge amounts of data in order to work but in relatively new fields, there is little data and thus, Artificial Intelligence can not replace humans in that regard.


However, this brings into question the reliability of Artificial Intelligence. The IB refers to reliability as the accuracy of data or its correspondence with the real world (International Baccalaureate). A significant aspect of Artificial Intelligence relies on the accuracy of data as well as it’s correspondence with the real world. If the data Artificial Intelligence uses in its machine learning is not reliable this has serious implications in many fields. Looking back at the example of predictive maintenance, Artificial Intelligence having inaccurate data will mean that machine downtime will be increased and businesses will be at a loss. To address such a problem, in developing Artificial Intelligence, much attention must be paid to the algorithms as well as the data. Humans can always involve themselves in data mining to ensure that the data that corresponds with the real world is always being used. This is another fact that counters the myth that Artificial Intelligence completely replaces humans, as it shows that Artificial Intelligence creates new jobs and opportunities for humans in these fields.


Additionally, another myth many believe about Artificial Intelligence is that it is objective and unbiased. Many are under the notion that Artificial Intelligence lacks subjectivity and is unprejudiced but this is not the case. As aforementioned, Artificial Intelligence requires a lot of data on a particular topic and at times this data may seem to be objective although it is not. For instance, in the human resources field, Artificial Intelligence is used to make decisions about which candidate is fit for a particular job. In such an instance, data about the ideal candidate and how past human resource managers made their decisions. However this data ends up becoming subjective. This is because the data is not only based on scientific evidence but also feelings and personal experiences. This ultimately influences the decision made by the Artificial Intelligence system and makes the decision bias. I am not so surprised to see that such a myth is false because if Artificial Intelligence was made to mimic the human brain how can an aspect such as bias be avoided?


Taking all things into consideration, it is clear that many preconceived notions about Artificial Intelligence come from the social and ethical concern of people and machines where people view Artificial Intelligence to be revolutionary, just like I did as a child, even to the extent of taking over the world. Artificial Intelligence is one of the most significant technological advancements I have ever seen. The sooner we debunk these myths, the better, because we will start to realize the numerous advantages Artificial Intelligence has in store for humans. I read a short story called “There Will Come Soft Rains” where a smart house was left standing after a war had taken all the humans off the face of the earth. It only took a day for the house to fall apart without the humans. Evidently, without humans technology would not exist and we need to comprehend that at the end of the day, the power rests in our hands.

It’s Only As Real As It Gets…

Try drawing this flower.


Was your drawing a 100% accurate? Would you consider it a good representation? These questions come up a lot during the design stage of the product development life cycle. The design stage is an important stage in the product development life cycle because it allows us to be focused, and efficient during the development stage. The design stage covers the relationships between the inputs, outputs, data structures and processes of the system represented in the form of models and design elements. Examples of these models and design elements include data flow diagrams, entity relationship diagrams and prototypes like wireframes and user interfaces.


In the post “The Importance of the Systems Development Life Cycle”, I spoke about the Exeat System my ITGS classmates and I tried to develop. As we designed our solution, I kept thinking about the concerns associated with using these computer models to achieve an accurate representation of the Exeat System. In this blog post, I will be sharing some challenges designers face when creating a computer model of some aspect of the world.


One challenge designers face when creating a computer model of some aspect of the world is the complexity of the real world. There are an infinite amount of possibilities in the world but it is hard to model that using technology. One way to overcome this is to make assumptions about the unknown possibilities. However, making assumptions can null the reliability of the model since it is not 100% accurate.


Moreover, another challenge faced by designers creating a computer model is the possibility that they may not have an understanding of how the real life system works to create a model. A typical example is the prediction of the occurrence of earthquakes. In reality, it is difficult to predict these and as a result, it will be difficult for a designer to create a model representing some aspect of the world.


Additionally, another challenge designers will face is the creation of a computer model relying heavily on research and past events. Designers will create a computer model that works effectively based on past and current information. The model will not be able to work effectively in the future as conditions may have changed. This reduces the effectiveness of the model and it will also not be a good representation of the real world.


All things considered, these are challenges that make the work of designers difficult as they create computer models of some aspect of the world. These challenges may be hard to overcome and may make the computer model slightly inaccurate, however, they are also proof that computer models can never accurately and perfectly represent the real world. Just like your drawing and the flower, there is always going to be that difference between the model and the real world. It’s only as real as it gets…
